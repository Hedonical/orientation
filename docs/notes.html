Why does this exist?

The goal of this piece is to allow the reader to triage what programming language, setup, tools, and processes would be best for them to answer novel questions. There are a bevy of excellent, free resources online that a person, no matter their education or background, can use to match the leading researchers and institutions. Paradoxically the vast number of these resources can lead to an information overload and paralysis, with a new or experienced researcher unsure of what path to take since they are so overwhelmed with the seemingly infinite possibilities.

While admittedly adding to this noise, this guide offers the resources that I have found the most compelling in my research career, which means they may or may not be useful for yourself. Academia is a field guilty of arbitrary gatekeeping and litmus tests against someone who simply has the curiosity to ask why, but this does a fundamental disservice to us all. Compelling research does not result from an academic title, prestigious institution, or specific place, it comes from someone who has the willingness to learn, share their unique perspectives, and have an equal review and conversation with their peers.

Since I equally love R & Python, I will discuss everything in this article through the process of doing things in either language. Even if you only use one, which you probably will, it doesn’t hurt to be familiar with what else is out there.

The Data Science Workflow

In my mind, the general tasks a data scientist performs from the inception to completion of a research project is as follows,

Data Acquisition 🔍 Data Processing 🚧 Data Storytelling 🎥

This the task of finding, scraping (if the data is not already structured), and loading a dataset. There are plenty of publicly accessible datasets you can export as a csv or other data type and load directly, but this data is commonly already analyzed or published as it’s the easiest to access. However, this in no way means the data is less relevant or valuable, it just means the author will have to combine it with more datasets or use a novel Data Storytelling method to add to contemporary literature.

If you already have a dataset that you want to analyze you only have to pay attention to the data loading part of this section.

Data processing or data wrangling is the process of cleaning, manipulating, and fundamentally changing the data you have from Data Acquisition.

This could mean something as simple summing a column of cocoa bean costs to find the total amount, or something as complex having three LLMs collectively read an unstructured text and vote on whether they believe the sentiment of the text is negative, neutral, or positive. The world is your oyster.

Whereas Data Acquisition and Data Processing would be the methods of a typical research paper, Data Storytelling is your conclusion and results. It is your opportunity to share with the world the magnum opus of your work, learning, and knowledge. Whether this takes the form of timelines, figures, or tables (usually a combination of all three), they should all contribute to a compelling narrative that anyone could follow, even if they are not a peer in your field.

Chefs pour their soul into a meal, artists their art piece, and a data scientist into their figures.

R 🟦 vs Python 🐍

R and Python shouldn’t be thought of as competitors but rather complimentary programming languages that have their own strengths and weaknesses. I have had many projects where I use Python and R. For example, I may use Python to pull and download datasets from a website, but then use R for the data processing and visuals.

While they have their own pros and cons, you should use the language that makes the most sense for you and critically the one that you can hold yourself accountable to consistently use. The best programming language at the end of the day is the language you can program in. This section will run through the most fundamental differences in syntax/workflows between these two languages.

This is the workflow where Python is far superior. Most public APIs have a python native version, making data acquisition and analysis a breeze.

On this note some notable Python packages to be aware of are:

https://pypi.org/project/requests/

Data Processing 🚧

This is where Python and R are evenly matched in terms of packages and capabilities. Although, polars is much faster and efficient than dplyr.

For Python

Polars

https://pypi.org/project/polars/

The best package for processing large datasets from my experience.

statsmodels

https://pypi.org/project/statsmodels/

Widely used package for statistical models and tests, but Polars will cover most of your needs.

For R

tidyverse - dplyr

https://dplyr.tidyverse.org/

The tidyverse is one mega package within R that contains almost everything you need to perform analysis start to finish, specifically for manipulating datasets the dplyr package is used. R also is nice because it has statistical models like linear regressions built within it.

Computer 💻 or Cloud ☁️ Setup

Before reading this section, please keep in mind that as with the rest of this article, no one approach is outright superior or better. Try and use the one that proves to have the least amount of friction for your brain and unique workflows.

The most fundamental difference between programming in R versus Python is the environment setup process, with Python being harder than R to setup but has more community built capabilities. Every programming language revolves around the interpreter (i.e. the low level code that parses the text we call Python or R and translates it into machine level code).

One more thing to keep in mind is the unsung hero of programming is good folder management, meaning you create folders with good but brief names, keep files in their proper folders, and

Computer 💻

The benefit of going down this route is that you will be able to theoretically run any package locally without an internet connection, unless the code itself requires one. The major downside of this route is you have to maintain your environments and keep things up to date. Also keep in mind code that works on your machine will probably not automatically work on someone else’s, but there are ways to standardize your code to run on any computer (just requires more work).

Note this requires use of your terminal, which is your way of directly interacting with your computer’s operating system.

Also note that you are at risk of losing your work if your computer dies, so make sure to do routine backups with an online data storage service. My recommendation is the dedicated free code storage tool named Github: https://docs.github.com/en/get-started/start-your-journey/about-github-and-git

You can also use Anaconda to ensure you do not have package conflicts but it is less pressing than Python. My personal recommendation is to at least use the pacman package in R since this streamlines package updates and install.

if (!"pacman" %in% installed.packages()){
install.packages("pacman")
}

# load pacman
library(pacman)

# load every other package with pacman, which will handle install and loading
